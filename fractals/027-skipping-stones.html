Fractal Nature of Water Ripples

When a stone skips across water, it creates a fractal pattern of interfering waves. Each impact generates concentric circular ripples that expand outward, and when multiple ripples meet, they create interference patterns with remarkable mathematical beauty. These patterns are fractal because the interference creates smaller ripples on top of larger ones, exhibiting self-similarity at different scales.

The physics of water waves follows the wave equation, where each point on the water surface oscillates up and down. When waves from different sources meet, they undergo constructive interference (waves add together) and destructive interference (waves cancel out). This creates complex patterns that look like nested circles, hyperbolas, and other geometric shapes - all emerging from simple circular waves.

The fractal dimension of these ripple patterns increases as more stones skip across the surface. A single stone creates simple concentric circles (dimension ≈ 1), but multiple skipping stones create intricate Moiré patterns and interference networks that approach a higher fractal dimension. The patterns are self-similar: zooming into any intersection point reveals similar interference structures at smaller scales.

Each skip in a stone's trajectory creates a new wave source, and the spacing between skips follows a natural decay pattern as the stone loses energy. This creates a hierarchical structure in the wave field: the first skip produces the largest ripples, subsequent skips produce progressively smaller ripples, creating a cascade of scales. Watch how the interference patterns become more complex as additional stones skip across the water, building fractal complexity through simple wave superposition.
